\subsection{The law across domains}
\label{sec:crossdomain}

Theorem~\ref{thm:limit} assumes a symmetric two-state \emph{Markov} capacity---so is
$\Delta(D)=\tfrac12 r(D)$ special to GPU bursting, or a property of acting on a delayed
observation as such? We test it on real binary states from other physical domains: an
acoustic source's azimuth sector (LOCATA microphone-array recordings) and a sperm-whale
coda-type sequence (Dominica), each observed at sensing age~$D$. For each we compare the
single-delayed-sample value
$\Delta_{\mathrm{pair}}(D)=\Pr[x_t{=}x_{t-D}]-\tfrac12=\tfrac12 r(D)$---our law evaluated
pointwise---against the value of the best predictor built from the delayed \emph{history}
(a six-tap Bayes rule, out of sample by five-fold contiguous block cross-validation). If
$x_{t-D}$ is a sufficient statistic---the Markov regime the theorem assumes---the history
cannot beat the single sample and $\tfrac12 r(D)$ is the full value of feedback, not a
lower bound. To separate real structure from finite-sample noise we calibrate with a
surrogate test: $120$ first-order Markov surrogates matched on each series' marginal and
lag-1 transition (Fig.~\ref{fig:crossdomain}, grey $95\%$ band).

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/confirm_crossdomain.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{$\Delta(D)=\tfrac12 r(D)$ across domains.} On real Markov-regime states
(source azimuth, whale coda type) and a synthetic Markov reference, the best
delayed-history policy (red, out-of-sample) stays on the single-lag law $\tfrac12 r(D)$
(blue) and inside the $95\%$ Markov-surrogate null band (grey): the law is exact, its
value set by the capacity autocorrelation at the sensing lag regardless of domain. The
i.i.d.\ control gives $\Delta=0$.}
\label{fig:crossdomain}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:crossdomain} confirms the law. On both real Markov-regime states and the
synthetic reference the delayed-history policy stays on $\tfrac12 r(D)$ and inside the
null band---the single-lag value is exact---while the controls behave as they must
(Markov: max-statistic $p{=}0.44$; i.i.d.: $\Delta=0$, $p{=}0.68$). The value of feedback
is thus governed by the capacity autocorrelation at the sensing lag independent of the
physical domain, confirming $\Delta(D)=\tfrac12 r(D)$ as a law of delayed observation
rather than an artifact of shared clusters. When a capacity is predictable beyond one lag
(non-Markov, e.g.\ bursty or rhythmic processes), the single-lag value is only a lower
bound that a history-using controller can exceed---the general converse is the
$\beta$-mixing bound of App.~\ref{app:beta}, and characterizing this predictive-information
regime is a natural extension.
