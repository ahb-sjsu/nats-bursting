\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

We evaluate \sysname{} on a production testbed: a home node and \ifblind a\else an Atlas\fi{}
workstation-class node running \texttt{nats-server} with JetStream enabled,
connected over three paths---co-located (loopback), Tailscale (mesh VPN), and a
single PAT-forwarded port (\texttt{:4222}). Unless stated otherwise the driver
issues 256\,B core-NATS request/reply and we report the mean and a $t$-based
95\% confidence interval over five independent temporal repeats. The study
answers seven questions:
\textbf{(Q1)} how the federated control path shapes latency and throughput;
\textbf{(Q2)} what JetStream durability costs;
\textbf{(Q3)} how the control plane scales with the in-flight window;
\textbf{(Q4)} the prober detection delay $D$ that governs the bound of
Prop.~\ref{prop:bound};
\textbf{(Q5)} whether the controller is polite-efficient under a self-imposed
budget (the \emph{abundance} regime);
\textbf{(Q6)} whether it holds under \emph{real, time-varying contention} (the
\emph{scarcity} regime Prop.~\ref{prop:bound} targets)---and where it fails; and
\textbf{(Q7)} whether the two-tier stack (macro admission $+$ micro thermal)
operates end-to-end on real hardware.

\subsection{Control-plane latency and throughput (Q1)}
\label{sec:eval-rtt}
Co-located, request/reply RTT is $0.84$\,ms (p50) at $6855$\,msg/s. Over
Tailscale---one WAN hop to the same responder---RTT rises to $76\pm13$\,ms (p50),
$171\pm96$ (p95), $302\pm170$ (p99), and closed-loop throughput at a 16-message
window falls to $188\pm28$\,msg/s. The federated path thus inflates median
control-RTT by roughly two orders of magnitude and throughput by an order of
magnitude. Crucially the cost is \emph{round-trip latency}, not server work:
a synchronous, per-message control loop would be RTT-bound. This is the direct
empirical motivation for the \emph{epoch-paced} admission controller of
\S\ref{sec:controller} rather than per-message acknowledgement, and for the
wide-tail variance ($95\%$ CI on p99 spans $\pm170$\,ms) that any WAN-side
controller must tolerate.

\subsection{Durability cost (Q2)}
\label{sec:eval-durability}
We compare JetStream publish-to-ack latency for file-backed and in-memory streams
over Tailscale. The medians are $81.4\pm10.6$\,ms (file) vs.\ $84.0\pm6.5$\,ms
(memory), overlapping at every percentile. Over this WAN, file persistence is
therefore statistically in\-dis\-tin\-guish\-able from in-memory: the round-trip
dominates the on-disk write, so durability is effectively free on the latency
axis. This is a useful negative result---it says the JetStream durable path can
be the default for the result channel without a measurable latency penalty.

\subsection{Control-plane scaling (Q3)}
\label{sec:eval-scaling}
Sweeping the in-flight window $w\in\{1,4,8,16,32\}$, throughput grows
sub-linearly: $11, 39, 83, 158, 283$\,msg/s respectively (95\% CIs widening from
$\pm2$ to $\pm128$). The curve is consistent with an RTT- and contention-bound
closed loop and with the AIMD window dynamics of \S\ref{sec:controller}: useful
in-flight work scales with the window until queueing and shared-link contention
flatten the gain---exactly the regime in which additive-increase probing and
multiplicative back-off are the appropriate control law.

\subsection{Detection delay and the violation bound (Q4)}
\label{sec:eval-detection}
$D$ has two measured components. The prober's per-call cost (read-only
\texttt{nvidia-smi}) is $27$--$33$\,ms (p50). The \emph{end-to-end} detection delay
---from launching a real GPU load until it is visible as utilization above the
$40\%$ floor (10 reps on a GV100, thermally guarded)---is \textbf{$2.46$\,s}
(mean; p50 $2.48$, range $2.29$--$2.61$), dominated by CUDA cold-start rather than
the poll. The relevant $D$ depends on the question: detecting a \emph{newly
admitted} pod's (un)productivity incurs the $\sim\!2.5$\,s cold-start term, while
probing \emph{already-running} pods costs only the $\sim\!27$\,ms poll.
Substituting into Prop.~\ref{prop:bound}, $\rho\approx D\alpha/((1-\beta)\bar a)$,
the $2.46$\,s term makes $D$ a \emph{first-order} contributor to the achievable
compliance---not a rounding error---which is precisely the systems$\leftrightarrow$theory
hinge the bound is meant to expose: a guest that admits aggressively (large
$\alpha$) pays for it in over-admission scaled by this measured cold-start delay.

\subsection{Politeness under abundance (Q5)}
\label{sec:eval-e2e}
We burst $B$ jobs under three policies---\emph{naive} (submit up to the hard cap),
\emph{static} (fixed submit rate), and \emph{aimd} (the completion-gated admission
controller of \S\ref{sec:controller})---against a self-imposed politeness budget
$C$, measuring goodput (tasks/s) and the over-budget fraction
$\rho$ (epochs with concurrency $>C$). To isolate the policy from the exogenous
scheduling and cold-image-pull variance of the shared cluster---which, reported
honestly, produced three pre-registered nulls in situ---we run on a controlled,
variance-free testbed (local-process workers on \ifblind the workstation node\else the Atlas node\fi; no scheduler or
registry jitter), pre-registered with randomized interleaved trial order and four
repeats per cell, at two scales (GPU pool $C{=}2$; CPU pool $C{=}8$).

Table~\ref{tab:e8} and Figs.~\ref{fig:pareto-gpu}--\ref{fig:pareto-cpu} report the
result. \textbf{aimd is the polite-efficient frontier point}: it holds the budget
exactly ($\rho=0$ at both scales) while delivering $1.72\times$ (GPU) and
$3.18\times$ (CPU) the goodput of static at the \emph{same} politeness, and it
matches naive's goodput without ever exceeding the budget. naive achieves its
goodput only by over-admitting ($\rho=0.54$ / $0.95$); static stays polite but
under-utilizes the budget. Both pre-registered hypotheses---H1 (aimd $>$ static
goodput at equal $\rho$) and H2 (naive impolite, $\rho>0$)---hold with disjoint
95\% CIs at both scales.

\emph{Scope.} This isolates the admission \emph{algorithm} on a controlled node;
the full pod-scheduling path (controller~$\to$~node-targeted GPU
worker~$\to$~federated result) is verified end-to-end on NRP Nautilus separately
(\S\ref{sec:impl}). Here $\bar a$ is a fixed self-imposed budget---the
\emph{abundance} regime, politeness as pure self-restraint. \S\ref{sec:eval-contended}
removes that assumption, driving $a_t$ with a real competing tenant so the
Prop.~\ref{prop:bound} bound is exercised under genuine \emph{scarcity}.

\subsection{Politeness under real contention (Q6)}
\label{sec:eval-contended}
Abundance tests self-restraint; the harder question is politeness under
\emph{scarcity}. We manufacture controlled contention on a two-GPU node
($C{=}2$): a \emph{competing tenant} occupies $c(t)\in\{0,1\}$ GPUs on a fixed
profile, so the guest's available capacity $a(t)=C-c(t)$ is exogenous,
time-varying, and \emph{measured} by the prober (a per-GPU process census, subject
to the detection delay $D$ of Q4)---the regime Prop.~\ref{prop:bound} targets. Two
profiles bracket the space: \emph{square} (a period-$2\tau$ wave, modelling
slowly-varying or scheduled load) and \emph{poisson} (memoryless on/off arrivals,
the adversarial case). Alongside goodput and $\rho$ we measure \emph{harm to the
neighbour}: the competitor's completed work relative to its undisturbed baseline.
Pre-registered (H1--H3), randomized interleaved order, four seeds per cell.

Table~\ref{tab:e9} and Fig.~\ref{fig:contended} report a sharper picture than
abundance did. \textbf{Under structured contention (square), aimd is the
polite-efficient knee}: $1.41\times$ static's goodput ($0.091$ vs $0.064$\,tasks/s,
disjoint CIs) at $\rho{=}0.15$---a $4\times$ lower over-admission than naive's
$\rho{=}0.64$---while sparing the neighbour ($76\%$ of its throughput retained vs
naive's $62\%$). naive's higher goodput ($0.101$) is bought \emph{entirely} at the
neighbour's expense: it over-admits two-thirds of the time and steals $38\%$ of the
competitor's throughput. H1 and H2 hold with disjoint CIs.

\textbf{Under unpredictable contention (poisson), feedback breaks even---and we
report it.} aimd's goodput ($0.062$) is statistically indistinguishable from
static's ($0.064$, overlapping CIs) and it over-admits \emph{more}
($\rho{=}0.30$), because capacity now varies on the timescale of the detection
delay: by the time the prober sees a competitor arrive, the observation is stale.
This is a pre-registered \emph{null} for H1, and it is precisely the failure
Prop.~\ref{prop:bound} predicts---$\rho$ grows with $D$ relative to the
capacity-variation timescale (slow square $\to\rho{=}0.15$; memoryless poisson
$\to\rho{=}0.30$). \textbf{(H3)} The measured detection path ($D\approx2$--$3$\,s,
Q4) places the Prop.~\ref{prop:bound} ceiling above the observed aimd $\rho$ in
both profiles: the bound is conservative for a discrete, completion-gated
controller, but its central prediction---politeness degrades as contention
outpaces $D$---is borne out. Practically: feedback admission pays when a
neighbour's load has structure to track, and one should fall back to a fixed budget
when it does not. naive is the most impolite in \emph{every} regime
($\rho{=}0.64$--$0.85$).

\subsection{Two-tier control on real hardware (Q7)}
\label{sec:eval-thermal}
The macro admission loop is paired with a \emph{micro} tier: the Kalman-filtered
thermal controller of \S\ref{sec:thermal}, run live during the burst. On the GPU
pool it is active throughout---throttling guest concurrency on $>\!90\%$ of epochs
under load (Fig.~\ref{fig:thermal})---and, tellingly, its throttling also
\emph{lowers} over-admission (aimd square $\rho$ falls $0.15\!\to\!0.08$ with the
micro tier engaged): both tiers push toward citizenship. Driven instead by CPU
temperature (its native actuator), the controller tracks a Kalman estimate and
throttles worker count predictively behind a hard safety cutout. On this
\emph{shared} node the result is itself a politeness lesson: the co-tenant baseline
($\sim$$77^\circ$C from the resident workload) sets a thermal floor that
self-throttling cannot undercut, and the controller correctly cuts out rather than
cook shared silicon. Even thermal headroom, on a shared machine, is set by one's
neighbours.

\subsection{Summary and threats to validity}
\label{sec:eval-threats}
The evaluation establishes: (i) the federated control path is RTT-bound (Q1),
motivating epoch-paced admission; (ii) JetStream durability is latency-free over
the WAN (Q2); (iii) control-plane throughput scales sub-linearly with the window
(Q3); (iv) the prober detection delay $D$ is a measured, first-order term in the
bound (Q4); (v) under abundance the controller is polite-efficient at both scales
(Q5); (vi) under \emph{structured} contention it is the polite-efficient knee and
spares the neighbour, while under \emph{memoryless} contention feedback breaks even
with static---a pre-registered null we report rather than suppress (Q6); and
(vii) the two-tier stack runs live on real hardware, both tiers pushing toward
citizenship (Q7). Principal threats: contention is manufactured on a two-GPU node
with $C{=}2$ and two synthetic profiles, not a production multi-tenant trace; the
poisson regime marks feedback's limit when capacity outpaces $D$; the GPU thermal
actuator (admission concurrency) is coarse and the shared node imposes a co-tenant
thermal floor; and federation interest/partition behaviour (E2/E6) and the
single-port traversal tax are characterized on one server or left to future work.
The full system path is nonetheless verified end-to-end, and naive's impoliteness
($\rho\ge0.64$, $\ge$$38\%$ neighbour harm) is robust across every regime.
