\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

We evaluate the controller on a testbed whose \emph{sensing age is measured, not
assumed}: a home node and \ifblind a\else an Atlas\fi{} workstation-class node with
GV100 GPUs, the controller probing real GPU occupancy and admitting real CUDA
workers, with a competing tenant driving exogenous capacity. The federated fabric
that carries this control plane---and its latency, durability, and scaling
characterization---is the companion systems paper's~\cite{companion}; here we take
the fabric as given and measure what the \emph{controller} does over it. Unless noted
we report the mean and a $t$-based 95\% confidence interval over independent repeats.
The study answers four questions:
\textbf{(Q1)} the prober detection delay $D$ that governs the bound of
Prop.~\ref{prop:bound};
\textbf{(Q2)} whether the controller is polite-efficient under a self-imposed
budget (the \emph{abundance} regime);
\textbf{(Q3)} whether it holds under \emph{real, time-varying contention} (the
\emph{scarcity} regime Prop.~\ref{prop:bound} targets)---and where it fails; and
\textbf{(Q4)} whether the feedback-usefulness law $\Delta(D)=\tfrac12 r(D)$
(Thm.~\ref{thm:limit}) holds at the controller level and unifies the two contention
regimes as points on one curve.

\subsection{Detection delay and the violation bound (Q1)}
\label{sec:eval-detection}
$D$ has two measured components. The prober's per-call cost (read-only
\texttt{nvidia-smi}) is $27$--$33$\,ms (p50). The \emph{end-to-end} detection delay
---from launching a real GPU load until it is visible as utilization above the
$40\%$ floor (10 reps on a GV100, thermally guarded)---is \textbf{$2.46$\,s}
(mean; p50 $2.48$, range $2.29$--$2.61$), dominated by CUDA cold-start rather than
the poll. The relevant $D$ depends on the question: detecting a \emph{newly
admitted} pod's (un)productivity incurs the $\sim\!2.5$\,s cold-start term, while
probing \emph{already-running} pods costs only the $\sim\!27$\,ms poll. In the
epoch-based model of \S\ref{sec:limit} one epoch is a single completion-gated
admission decision, so the cold-start-dominated age maps to $D\!\approx\!3$ epochs
---the value swept in the law validation (Q4)---while the $27$\,ms poll is sub-epoch
for already-running pods.
Substituting into Prop.~\ref{prop:bound}, $\rho\approx D\alpha/((1-\beta)\bar a)$,
the $2.46$\,s term makes $D$ a \emph{first-order} contributor to the achievable
compliance---not a rounding error---which is precisely the systems$\leftrightarrow$theory
hinge the bound is meant to expose: a guest that admits aggressively (large
$\alpha$) pays for it in over-admission scaled by this measured cold-start delay.

\subsection{Politeness under abundance (Q2)}
\label{sec:eval-e2e}
We burst $B$ jobs under three policies---\emph{naive} (submit up to the hard cap),
\emph{static} (fixed submit rate), and \emph{aimd} (the completion-gated admission
controller of \S\ref{sec:controller})---against a self-imposed politeness budget
$C$, measuring goodput (tasks/s) and the over-budget fraction
$\rho$ (epochs with concurrency $>C$). To isolate the policy from the exogenous
scheduling and cold-image-pull variance of the shared cluster---which, reported
honestly, produced three pre-registered nulls in situ---we run on a controlled,
variance-free testbed (local-process workers on \ifblind the workstation node\else the Atlas node\fi; no scheduler or
registry jitter), pre-registered with randomized interleaved trial order and four
repeats per cell, at two scales (GPU pool $C{=}2$; CPU pool $C{=}8$).

Table~\ref{tab:e8} and Figs.~\ref{fig:pareto-gpu}--\ref{fig:pareto-cpu} report the
result. \textbf{aimd is the polite-efficient frontier point}: it holds the budget
exactly ($\rho=0$ at both scales) while delivering $1.72\times$ (GPU) and
$3.18\times$ (CPU) the goodput of static at the \emph{same} politeness, and it
matches naive's goodput without ever exceeding the budget. naive achieves its
goodput only by over-admitting ($\rho=0.54$ / $0.95$); static stays polite but
under-utilizes the budget. Both pre-registered hypotheses---H1 (aimd $>$ static
goodput at equal $\rho$) and H2 (naive impolite, $\rho>0$)---hold with disjoint
95\% CIs at both scales.

\emph{Scope.} This isolates the admission \emph{algorithm} on a controlled node;
the full pod-scheduling path (controller~$\to$~node-targeted GPU
worker~$\to$~federated result) is verified end-to-end on NRP Nautilus separately
(\S\ref{sec:impl}). Here $\bar a$ is a fixed self-imposed budget---the
\emph{abundance} regime, politeness as pure self-restraint. \S\ref{sec:eval-contended}
removes that assumption, driving $a_t$ with a real competing tenant so the
Prop.~\ref{prop:bound} bound is exercised under genuine \emph{scarcity}.

\subsection{Politeness under real contention (Q3)}
\label{sec:eval-contended}
Abundance tests self-restraint; the harder question is politeness under
\emph{scarcity}. We manufacture controlled contention on a two-GPU node
($C{=}2$): a \emph{competing tenant} occupies $c(t)\in\{0,1\}$ GPUs on a fixed
profile, so the guest's available capacity $a(t)=C-c(t)$ is exogenous,
time-varying, and \emph{measured} by the prober (a per-GPU process census, subject
to the detection delay $D$ of Q1)---the regime Prop.~\ref{prop:bound} targets. Two
profiles bracket the space: \emph{square} (a period-$2\tau$ wave, modelling
slowly-varying or scheduled load) and \emph{poisson} (memoryless on/off arrivals,
the adversarial case). Alongside goodput and $\rho$ we measure \emph{harm to the
neighbour}: the competitor's completed work relative to its undisturbed baseline.
Pre-registered (H1--H3), randomized interleaved order, four seeds per cell.

Table~\ref{tab:e9} and Fig.~\ref{fig:contended} report a sharper picture than
abundance did. \textbf{Under structured contention (square), aimd is the
polite-efficient knee}: $1.41\times$ static's goodput ($0.091$ vs $0.064$\,tasks/s,
disjoint CIs) at $\rho{=}0.15$---a $4\times$ lower over-admission than naive's
$\rho{=}0.64$---while sparing the neighbour ($76\%$ of its throughput retained vs
naive's $62\%$). The target $\epsilon$ is grounded in the host policy: NRP acts on
\emph{persistently} under-utilized requests rather than momentary dips below the
$\ge\!40\%$ GPU floor, so $\epsilon$ is the tolerable share of epochs a pod may sit
under-utilized; read that way the $\rho{=}0.15$ aimd holds is a deliberately
conservative operating point, inside a $\sim\!0.1$--$0.2$ band consistent with that
floor at our epoch length. naive's higher goodput ($0.101$) is bought \emph{entirely} at the
neighbour's expense: it over-admits two-thirds of the time and steals $38\%$ of the
competitor's throughput. H1 and H2 hold with disjoint CIs.

\textbf{Under unpredictable contention (poisson), feedback breaks even---and we
report it.} aimd's goodput ($0.062$) is statistically indistinguishable from
static's ($0.064$, overlapping CIs) and it over-admits \emph{more}
($\rho{=}0.30$), because capacity now varies on the timescale of the detection
delay: by the time the prober sees a competitor arrive, the observation is stale.
This is a pre-registered \emph{null} for H1, and it is precisely what
Thm.~\ref{thm:limit} predicts: the two profiles are two coherence times on the
$\Delta(D)=\tfrac12 r(D)$ curve (Cor.~\ref{cor:exp}), with the slow square
($\tau_c\!\gg\!D$) giving feedback room to help and the memoryless poisson
($\tau_c\!\lesssim\!D$) driving $r(D)\!\to\!0$, where no causal controller can beat
static---so feedback's break-even is a theorem, not a tuning failure. \textbf{(H3)}
The measured detection path ($D\approx2$--$3$\,s, Q1) also places the
Prop.~\ref{prop:bound} sawtooth ceiling above the observed aimd $\rho$ in both
profiles: conservative for a discrete, completion-gated controller, but its
prediction---politeness degrades as contention outpaces $D$---is borne out.
Practically, this is exactly the case for the regime-adaptive controller
(\S\ref{sec:controller}, Thm.~\ref{thm:adapt}): track $r(D)$ and fall back to a
fixed budget when the neighbour has no structure to exploit. naive is the most
impolite in \emph{every} regime ($\rho{=}0.64$--$0.85$).

\subsection{Validating the feedback-usefulness law (Q4)}
\label{sec:eval-law}
The contention results above are two points; Thm.~\ref{thm:limit} predicts the whole
curve. To test it we simulate the actual window controllers on the two-state
capacity process the theory analyzes---a Markov competitor with per-epoch flip
probability $p$, so the coherence time $\tau_c=-1/\ln(1-2p)$ sweeps continuously
(matching \texttt{contention.py --profile markov})---at the measured sensing age
$D{=}3$ epochs, $1.2{\times}10^{5}$ epochs per point. Fig.~\ref{fig:tausweep}
reports the goodput advantage of each controller over the best static budget at
matched over-admission, against the predicted $\Delta(D)=\tfrac12 r(D)$.

Three facts confirm the theory and motivate the adaptive controller.
\textbf{(i)} The delayed-threshold (Bayes-optimal) policy sits \emph{on} the
predicted line across three decades of $\tau_c$---maximum deviation
$|\Delta_{\text{opt}}-\tfrac12 r(D)|=0.013$---so the closed form is not an asymptote
but the exact frontier. \textbf{(ii)} Practical AIMD captures roughly half the
available advantage when $\tau_c\!\gg\!D$ and, crucially, goes \emph{negative}
(worse than static) once $\tau_c\!\lesssim\!D$: it keeps probing a signal that has
become noise, paying over-admission for nothing---the same failure the live poisson
regime shows. \textbf{(iii)} The regime-adaptive controller, estimating $r(D)$
online, tracks the upper envelope: it never drops below static and recovers most of
AIMD's advantage where feedback is worth having. Finally, sweeping $D$ collapses
every curve onto $\tfrac12 e^{-D/\tau_c}$ when plotted against $D/\tau_c$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:tausweep}, right), confirming the predicted scaling. The live
square/poisson pair (Q3) are the two ends of this axis: $\tau_c\!\gg\!D$ (feedback
wins) and $\tau_c\!\lesssim\!D$ (feedback breaks even), exactly as
Cor.~\ref{cor:exp} states.

\subsection{Summary and threats to validity}
\label{sec:eval-threats}
The evaluation establishes: (i) the prober detection delay $D$ is a measured,
first-order term in the bound---$\approx2.5$\,s, CUDA-cold-start-dominated (Q1);
(ii) under abundance the controller is polite-efficient at both scales, $1.7$--$3.2\times$
static's goodput at $\rho{=}0$ (Q2); (iii) under \emph{structured} contention it is
the polite-efficient knee and spares the neighbour, while under \emph{memoryless}
contention feedback breaks even with static---a pre-registered null we report rather
than suppress (Q3); and (iv) the feedback-usefulness law $\Delta(D)=\tfrac12 r(D)$
holds at the controller level, the optimal policy sitting on it to within $0.013$
while the regime-adaptive controller never falls below static (Q4), unifying the two
live contention regimes as endpoints of one curve. Principal threats: contention is
manufactured on a two-GPU node with $C{=}2$ and two synthetic profiles, not a
production multi-tenant trace; and the law validation (Q4) and the regime-adaptive
controller are established in controller-level simulation on the theory's two-state
process, with the live cluster run of the adaptive policy left as the next anchor.
The full system path is nonetheless verified end-to-end (\S\ref{sec:impl}), and
naive's impoliteness ($\rho\ge0.64$, $\ge$$38\%$ neighbour harm) is robust across
every regime.
